Forces in Biology

👂
Mechanosensing & Hearing
How biomolecules detect and interpret tiny mechanical inputs. Sound-induced vibrations create molecular-scale forces that stretch specialized protein complexes in the inner ear — nanoscale force sensors that make hearing possible.
⚙️
Force-Generating Machines
Motor proteins walking along cellular tracks, contractile systems generating tension, and dynamic protein assemblies reorganizing cellular architecture — biological machines that produce and harness force to perform work.
🔬
Bio-Inspired Nanomotors
Translating biology's design strategies into engineered systems. Our recent work develops actively spinning, self-driven microscopic structures — bio-compatible engines as smart, controllable drug carriers.
// Deeper Dive

Research Themes

How does the ear sense both whispers and music? +
The inner ear contains protein complexes that behave like nanoscale springs. These respond differently depending on how strongly and how quickly they are pulled — allowing the same molecular machinery to detect both the faintest murmur and the loudest sound. Our lab uses single-molecule force spectroscopy to characterize how these proteins' mechanical properties encode acoustic information at the molecular scale.
What makes a good molecular motor? +
We study how motor proteins convert chemical energy (from ATP hydrolysis) into directional mechanical work. By applying controlled forces to single motor molecules and measuring their stepping behavior, we uncover the design principles that make these machines so efficient — and use those principles to engineer synthetic nanomotors.
How can cancer metastasis be inhibited? +
Our Wellcome/DBT-funded research identified an atypical cell-cell adhesion protein that inhibits cancer metastasis. Understanding the mechanical forces governing cell adhesion opens pathways to controlling how cancer cells detach from primary tumors and invade distant tissues.
What are bio-compatible nanoscale engines? +
Inspired by cellular motor proteins, we develop actively spinning and self-propelled microscopic structures made from biocompatible materials. These nano-engines can be controlled externally and hold promise as drug delivery vehicles that navigate the body's complex environment with precision.
